Course Description

This course explores the application of AI in the social sciences, using computational models to replicate complex social behavior that emerges from individual interactions — examining cooperation, conflict, and collective outcomes. Students gain hands-on experience with Agent-Based Modeling (ABM) and LLM APIs, and run in-class social experiments (decision-making games) to see how models capture — and fall short of — real human behavior.

A core case study is the urban model: although residents prefer medium density, cities still tend to overcrowd — highlighting the tension between individual and collective rationality. The semester progresses from basic ABM and parameter analysis to LLM-driven simulations, emphasizing creativity, teamwork, and independent inquiry.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of the course, students will be able to:

  • Read and understand social-behavior modeling code with AI-assisted tools.
  • Apply agent-based modeling (ABM) to simulate group dynamics and social systems.
  • Understand LLM fundamentals and use APIs to design simple experiments via prompt engineering.
  • Collaborate in teams on a social-simulation project — data collection, design, analysis, and reporting.
  • Critically evaluate the potential and ethical implications of AI in social science.

Prerequisites / Co-requisites

No formal prerequisites or co-requisites. Basic Python familiarity is helpful but not required — the course builds the necessary skills as it progresses.

ComponentWeight

Lab Engagement (40%)

Four practical lab assignments across the semester, each worth 10%. Each is designed to be completed within a lab session (about one hour of work).

Midterm Project (30%)

A group presentation synthesizing and comparing the previous labs into a cohesive slide deck, with each member describing their contributions. Bonus points for deeper exploration or modification of the simulation models.

Final Project (30%)

A group presentation and report synthesizing the ABM variants studied before the midterm with the LLM-driven techniques introduced afterward, summarizing key results and offering critical reflections.
